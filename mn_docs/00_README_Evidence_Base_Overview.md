# Minnesota Medicaid Program Integrity Evidence Base
### Prepared to support the design of a Washington State public-data review-priority framework for Adult Family Homes & HCBS
**Compiled by Mohamed Noor Hussein | June 2026**

## Purpose
This evidence base compiles publicly available, verifiable data documenting Minnesota's 2024–2026 Medicaid program-integrity enforcement record. It is intended as a structured reference for designing a public-data review-priority framework for Washington State, with a focus on Adult Family Homes (AFHs) and Home and Community-Based Services (HCBS).

## Evidentiary Standards
This package strictly distinguishes between categories of conduct. **Charged or alleged cases are accusations only; all defendants are presumed innocent until proven guilty.** "Improper payments" (administrative errors) are distinguished from "fraud" (intentional deception). Audit findings and administrative disenrollments (e.g., for incomplete paperwork) are not findings of fraud. Every factual claim is traceable to an official source listed in the bibliography.

## Key Findings at a Glance
1. **The crisis reflected a gap in proactive oversight, not a law-enforcement failure.** Minnesota auditors described the same structural gaps (no financial documentation at licensing, reactive-only investigations) as early as 2017, but the recommendations were not implemented before the enforcement wave began.
2. **Unexplained spending growth was the clearest early signal.** New HCBS waiver services — HSS (projected $2.6M/yr, reached $104M), EIDBI ($600K to over $400M), and IHS ($100M to $700M) — grew at rates that patient-need alone does not explain.
3. **Frontline warnings were ignored.** County investigators explicitly told the state they were "flying blind" on housing fraud a full year before administrative action was taken.
4. **The federal consequences were severe.** CMS threatened to withhold up to $2 billion in funding, forcing the "Revalidate 2026" effort that disenrolled ~3,400 high-risk providers.
